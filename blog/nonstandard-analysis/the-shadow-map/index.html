The Shadow Map Theorem

Theorem: Every limited hyperreal number $a \in {^* \mathbb{R}}$ is infinitely close to a unique real number. This is called the shadow of $a$, or the standard part of $a$, typically denoted as follows

\[sh(a) = st(a) = {^{\circ} a}\]

Corollary: Every limited hyperreal number $a \in {^* \mathbb{R}}$ can be expressed as $a = r + \epsilon$ where $r$ is a real number (namely $sh(a)$) and $\epsilon$ is an infinitesimal.

Properties

For these proofs, let $x = r_1 + \epsilon_1$ and $y = r_2 + \epsilon_2$ where $r_1 = sh(x)$ and $r_2 = sh(y)$.

Addition

\[\begin{align} sh(x + y) &= sh( (r_1 + \epsilon_1) + (r_2 + \epsilon_2) ) \\[10pt] &= sh( (r_1 + r_2) + (\epsilon_1 + \epsilon_2) ) \\[10pt] &= r_1 + r_2 \\[10pt] &= sh(x) + sh(y) \end{align}\]

Multiplication

\[\begin{align} sh(x \cdot y) &= sh( (r_1 + \epsilon_1) \cdot (r_2 + \epsilon_2) ) \\[10pt] &= sh( (r_1 \cdot r_2) + (r_1 \cdot \epsilon_2 + r_2 \cdot \epsilon_1 + \epsilon_1 \cdot \epsilon_2) ) \\[10pt] &= sh( (r_1 \cdot r_2) + \epsilon_3 ) \\[10pt] &= r_1 \cdot r_2 \\[10pt] &= sh(x) \cdot sh(y) \end{align}\]

As a corollary, if $x = r$ where $r \in \mathbb{R}$ is a pure real number, then

\[sh(r \cdot y) = sh(r) \cdot sh(y) = r \cdot sh(y)\]

Thus, as an additional corollary, weâ€™ve shown the linearity of the shadow map.

\[sh(\alpha x + \beta y) = \alpha sh(x) + \beta sh(y)\]

As a final corollary, taking $\alpha = 1$ and $\beta = -1$, we get the subtraction law

\[sh(x - y) = sh(x) - sh(y)\]

Reciprocation

Assume that $sh(x) \neq 0$.

\[\begin{align} sh \left (\frac{1}{x} \right ) &= sh \left ( \frac{1}{r_1 + \epsilon_1} \right ) \\[10pt] &= sh \left ( \frac{1}{r_1} + \frac{\epsilon_1}{r_1 (r_1 + \epsilon_1)} \right ) \\[10pt] &= sh \left ( \frac{1}{r_1} + \epsilon_3 \right ) \\[10pt] &= \frac{1}{r_1} \\[10pt] &= \frac{1}{sh(x)} \end{align}\]

Division

Assume that $sh(y) \neq 0$. We just combine the multiplication and reciprocation properties.

\[\begin{align} sh \left (\frac{x}{y} \right ) &= sh \left (x \cdot \frac{1}{y} \right ) \\[10pt] &= sh(x) \cdot sh \left (\frac{1}{y} \right ) \\[10pt] &= sh(x) \cdot \frac{1}{sh(y)} \\[10pt] &= \frac{sh(x)}{sh(y)} \end{align}\]
